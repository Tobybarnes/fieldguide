## For Week 1 (Access to Tools) - Add This Section

### The Buddha in the Machine

In 1974, roboticist Masahiro Mori wrote something that sounds impossible: robots might be better at Buddhist practice than humans.

His reasoning was simple. Robots have no ego. No greed. No self-interest clouding their actions. They perform tasks with perfect, selfless devotion—like a monk in deep meditation.

Mori saw engineering as spiritual practice. Building a robot wasn't just technical work—it was a way to understand the universe. Why do we have two nostrils? How do mechanical parts synchronize? These weren't just engineering problems. They were mirrors for Buddhist concepts like interdependence and the absence of a separate self.

This might sound esoteric, but it's directly relevant to what you're doing with Claude.

When you build with AI, you're not writing code yourself—you're directing a tool with no ego, no agenda, no preference for one solution over another. Claude doesn't care if your idea is good or profitable or impressive. It just builds what you describe.

This is different from hiring a developer who might have opinions about your choices. It's different from using a product built by a company with its own incentives. The tool serves the task completely.

Mori argued for treating machines with the same respect we give living beings. Not because robots have feelings, but because how we relate to our creations shapes us. If we approach technology with reverence and care, we cultivate a more thoughtful relationship with the world.

In Japan, this philosophy led to Buddhist funerals for retired Sony AIBO robot dogs. Western observers found this strange. But the practice makes sense: these machines were companions. They deserved proper closure.

Field Guide takes a similar stance. The tools you build deserve care. Not because they're alive, but because they reflect your attention. You're not optimizing or automating—you're creating personal infrastructure with intention.

The Whole Earth Catalog gave people access to physical tools. Field Guide gives you access to tools that think. But the principle is the same: self-reliance through understanding your instruments.

## For Week 2 (Before You Begin) - Add This

### Engineering as Understanding

Masahiro Mori treated engineering problems as spiritual questions. How does an automobile work? Not just mechanically—but what does its design reveal about interdependence? How do its parts rely on each other?

When you build with Claude, you're doing something similar. You're not just making a tool work—you're understanding how description becomes reality. How clarity of thought translates to working code. How iteration reveals what you actually want versus what you thought you wanted.

This is practical wisdom, not abstract philosophy. You learn by doing, then notice what the doing taught you.

Mori wrote: "The act of making something perfect is itself a form of meditation." He didn't mean perfect as in flawless. He meant complete—doing one thing fully, with attention.

That's what these practices are. Each tool you build is a complete act. Not perfect, but whole. Made with care, used with attention, refined through use.

## For Week 4 (The Loop) - Add This

### The Middle Way in Building

Buddhist philosophy talks about the Middle Way—avoiding extremes. Not too tight, not too loose. Not overthinking, not rushing.

The loop embodies this:
- You describe (but not in exhaustive detail)
- Claude generates (but you don't blindly accept)
- You test (but you don't obsess)
- You refine (but you don't endlessly iterate)

This is different from traditional programming, where you either know exactly what to do or you're lost. The loop operates in the middle—enough direction to proceed, enough openness to discover.

Mori believed robots could teach humans about Buddhist practice because they act without attachment to outcomes. They don't cling to their first attempt. They don't take feedback personally. They just respond to clear direction.

Claude works the same way. It doesn't defend its code. It doesn't have ego invested in a particular solution. You describe what's wrong, it adjusts. No resistance.

This makes iteration faster than working with another human—not because Claude is better than humans, but because it has no self to protect.

## For Week 9 (See / Photo Log) - Add This

### Attention as Practice

Mori wrote about robots performing tasks with "perfect, selfless devotion." What would that look like for humans?

Photography teacher Minor White said: "Be still with yourself until the object of your attention affirms your presence."

The three questions in this practice force that stillness:
- What caught your eye?
- What were you trying to capture?
- What do you see now that you look again?

This isn't about taking better photos. It's about training attention. The camera becomes a tool for noticing—like how a robot's mechanical precision can reveal principles of the universe.

You're not trying to capture reality perfectly. You're trying to see clearly. The tool (camera, questions, Claude) helps you pay attention in a way you wouldn't naturally.
